About Cassava Cassava is a very tough root crop that can survive in dry areas where other crops fail. It is a major food security crop in Western Kenya, Coast Region, and Eastern Kenya. Cassava is eaten boiled, roasted, or ground into flour. It is sometimes called the "drought crop" because it survives hard times.
📅 When to Plant
  • Best planted at the start of the rainy season: March or October
  • Can also be planted in dry season with some irrigation
  • Grows in areas with as little as 500mm of rainfall per year
  • Takes 9 to 18 months to reach full maturity
  • Can stay in the ground for up to 2 years without rotting

>🌱 Planting Stages
  1. Stem Cutting Selection – Choose healthy stems from a mature cassava plant; cut into 20–30cm pieces
  2. Land Preparation – Dig and loosen soil well; make mounds or ridges
  3. Planting – Push cuttings into mounds at an angle; 2–3 buds must be above ground
  4. Sprouting – New leaves emerge from buds in 1–3 weeks
  5. Vegetative Stage – Plant grows tall with large leaves; roots develop underground
  6. Root Thickening – Roots grow larger and fill with starch (months 4–12)
  7. Maturity – Leaves start yellowing; roots are large and starchy
  8. Harvest – Dig up roots carefully; done from 9 months up to 2 years

🦠 Common Diseases
  • Cassava Mosaic Virus – Yellow mosaic pattern on leaves; very common in Kenya; spread by whiteflies
  • Cassava Brown Streak – Yellow streaks on leaves; brown rot inside the root
  • Bacterial Blight – Water-soaked spots on leaves; stem oozes gum
  • Root Rot – Caused by waterlogging; roots turn soft and smelly
Prevention: Use disease-free cuttings, plant resistant varieties, control whiteflies. 🐛 Common Pests
  • Whiteflies – Spread cassava mosaic virus; major pest
  • Cassava Mealybug – White fluffy insects that suck sap; stunt growth
  • Green Mite – Tiny mites that make leaves curl and dry
  • Grasshoppers – Eat leaves, especially in dry areas
Control: Use resistant varieties, biological control, and remove infected plants.
⭐ Why Cassava is Important
  • A food security crop — survives droughts when maize fails
  • Can be stored in the ground and harvested when needed
  • Used to make flour, chips, starch, and animal feed
  • Grows with very low inputs — no expensive fertilisers needed
  • Important for communities in Western and Coastal Kenya
